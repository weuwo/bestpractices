BEST PRACTICES

an artwork by Lauren Leung Cramer

Best Practices is a website and interactive installation that harkens to early 2000s bedroom nostalgia. By searching for and indexing personal objects, visitors unearth anecdotes from the artist’s own life, collectively forming a larger narrative.

Best Practices is the feeling of sifting through the silt of the past and uncovering memories piece by piece. My childhood memories are elusive. As a result of complex events in my early years, unconventional family dynamics, and a genetic history of memory loss, these memories do not come easily, and I often rely on physical memorabilia in the present to transport me back into the world of the past. Over the years, I have developed an attachment to collecting trinkets and tchotchkes. These objects feed the desires of my “inner child”; they represent a time of my life that I still yearn for and feel part of through these simple means.


HOW TO PLAY

Click and drag the mouse to move through the environment and look for hidden objects. Toggle the index to see all found/missing objects and their descriptions. Find all 9 objects to restart the game.

In Ocumicho, Mexican pottery artists lean into the surreal through mischievous and playful depictions of the devil. Before I was born, my father was mustachioed and drove thousands of miles in a Volkswagen bus that didn't look too far off from this one. In his travels, he picked up this ceramic sculpture. For years it sat atop the bookshelf in my childhood bedroom: a blue devil with his head in the clouds. Those, too, were my father's doing. With an airbrush and a delicate hand, he painted fluffy white clouds on my cerulean ceiling so I would always be looking at the sky. But with the devil looking down at me, I was never quite at ease.

Gwun Yam (觀音 , or Avalokiteśvara) is the Buddhist bodhisattva of compassion; depicted often as a woman, sometimes touting an infant son on her arm. Despite her striking resemblance and common comparison to the Virgin Mary, the two were depicted centuries and oceans apart - it is a scene that transcends cultural and religious bounds.


Growing up, I said goodnight to Gwun Yum and her baby every night. "Give them a kiss" my mother would say, and I'd plant a smooch on each of their foreheads, thanking them for protecting me (from the monsters under my bed.)

If you've never had a pineapple guava, you must do everything in your capacity to change that. They grow by the bucketsful in my grandma's backyard and fit perfectly in the palm of your hand, skin a velvety olive green. When perfectly ripe, they fall to the ground, and as a child I would collect them in a basket, greedy for the tropical, jammy taste of my favorite summer fruit. You can slice them in half longways and dig the flesh out with a spoon, the rounded metal contour a perfect match for their shape. My best friend Lian called them "guayabas” and she and I would sit on my grandma's patio swing, kicking our legs and scraping them clean, guava after guava.

My mom bought a house after my parents' divorce: it was beige and flat with a perfectly rectangular backyard. I remember rummaging in the soil there, not fully able to process that a place which felt so foreign now belonged only to us two. Beneath a parched and leafless bush, I found a chunk of quartz caked in dirt. I held it up to the sun and rotated it in my palm, watching the light refract on its solid faces. Crystal Quartz supposedly symbolizes cleansing and purifying of energy. It felt, at the time, like a treasure left behind for me to find in a suburban plot that was no one's in particular.

If a picture is with a thousand words, a diary is worth a thousand pictures. Chock-full with recounts of all of my crushes, hang-ups, obsessions, and fantasies between the ages of 7 and 13, nothing paints a picture of the past as clearly as this. At the time, it was my relief from a world where my body and mind were developing at rapid speeds to scrawl out my every thought. I have maintained a habit, still, of keeping a diary, although more so as a gift to my future self. While the fear of my unruly memory can at times feel paralyzing, there is comfort in words on paper which are so permanent.

An Aquapet is essentially a phallic snowglobe that plays music. There is a mechanism that puppeteers a small plastic alien encapsulated inside in such a way that will secure the attention of a child for at least a day, at most a week. But Aquapets are born to die - they are the epitome of planned obsolescence, as once the small amount of water inside of them evaporates, the alien will sink the the bottom, never to dance again. One might call it my earliest concept of impermanence.
